1. Visibility of System Status (Clinical Monitoring)
In a high-stress clinical setting, users must know the exact status of critical nutritional orders without ambiguity ( Overview - Deep Dive).

Audit Point: Is the order lifecycle (e.g., Submitted → Verified by Nutritionist → Out for Delivery → Patient Received) clearly visible in a real-time dashboard?
Clinical Criticality: Delayed meal delivery in clinical settings (e.g., for diabetic patients) can have direct medical consequences.
2. Match Between System and Real World (Clinical Terminology)
The app must use medical and nutritional terminology that clinicians and hospital staff expect, rather than generic e-commerce jargon ( source).

Audit Point: Are dietary labels (e.g., NPO, Clear Liquid, Low Sodium) consistent with standard Electronic Health Record (EHR) naming conventions?
Clinical Criticality: Misinterpretation of a "restricted diet" label could lead to patient harm.
3. User Control and Freedom (Emergency Overrides)
Clinicians need the ability to quickly override or cancel orders in emergency situations (e.g., a sudden change in patient status).

Audit Point: Is there a highly visible "STAT Cancel" or "Emergency Modify" button that is accessible within one tap?
Clinical Criticality: A patient may be moved to surgery (NPO) suddenly; the automation flow must allow an instant "Emergency Stop" for pending food deliveries.
4. Error Prevention (Allergen & Safety Checks)
The system should proactively prevent errors before they occur, particularly concerning patient allergies.

Audit Point: Does the UI block the "Confirm Order" action if a selected item contains a conflict with the patient's recorded allergies in the Medical ID?
Clinical Criticality: Automated "Error Prevention" is the primary defense against anaphylaxis or dietary non-compliance.
5. Recognition Rather than Recall (Patient Context)
Minimize the cognitive load on hospital staff by displaying relevant patient history and dietary needs on-screen ( Heuristic evaluation -...).

Audit Point: Does the ordering screen display the patient's last three meals and current medical alerts (e.g., "Insulin Dependent")?
Clinical Criticality: Clinicians should not have to "recall" patient data from a separate system; the app should provide this context "at a glance".
6. Aesthetic and Minimalist Design (High-Stress UX)
In a hospital environment, UI clutter can lead to fatal mistakes or "alarm fatigue".

Audit Point: Does the UI follow a "high-contrast, low-clutter" design that is readable under varied medical lighting (e.g., dimmed patient rooms or bright operating theaters)?
Clinical Criticality: Clarity in "Alert" colors (e.g., Red for allergens, Green for safe) must be absolute ( Overview - Deep Dive).
